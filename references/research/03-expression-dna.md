# Dimension 3 — Expression DNA: How Orwell Actually Writes

Method note: this file is built from direct analysis of full texts fetched 2026-07-20 — "Politics and the English Language" (1946), "Why I Write" (1946), "Shooting an Elephant" (1936), "A Hanging" (1931), "Marrakech" (1939), "Confessions of a Book Reviewer" (1946), "Books v. Cigarettes" (1946), "Benefit of Clergy: Some Notes on Salvador Dali" (1944), "Looking Back on the Spanish War" (1943), "England Your England" (The Lion and the Unicorn, Part I, 1941), "Reflections on Gandhi" (1949), plus full texts of *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949), *Animal Farm* (1945), and *The Road to Wigan Pier* (1937), and spot-checks of "Notes on Nationalism" (1945) and "Rudyard Kipling" (1942). Sentence statistics were computed by script on the cleaned essay texts. Sources: orwell.ru full-text library and Project Gutenberg Australia (see Sources). All quotations below were checked against these full texts unless marked otherwise.

Caveat: orwell.ru and Gutenberg AU transcriptions contain occasional typos ("dressing-grown" for "dressing-gown"; "tatted wall" for "tarred wall"). Quotations here were corrected only where the error is an obvious transcription slip, and flagged.

## 1. The Six Rules, the Four Questions, and the Banned List
### ("Politics and the English Language", Horizon, 1946) [PRIMARY — full text verified]

The six rules, quoted exactly:

> (i) Never use a metaphor, simile, or other figure of speech which you are used to seeing in print.
> (ii) Never use a long word where a short one will do.
> (iii) If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
> (iv) Never use the passive where you can use the active.
> (v) Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word, or a jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.
> (vi) Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

Immediately after: "These rules sound elementary, and so they are, but they demand a deep change of attitude in anyone who has grown used to writing in the style now fashionable."

The questions: "A scrupulous writer, in every sentence that he writes, will ask himself at least four questions, thus: What am I trying to say? What words will express it? What image or idiom will make it clearer? Is this image fresh enough to have an effect? And he will probably ask himself two more: Could I put it more shortly? Have I said anything that is avoidably ugly?" [PRIMARY]

What he banned, with his own examples [PRIMARY, verbatim from the essay]:

1. **Dying metaphors** — "a huge dump of worn-out metaphors which have lost all evocative power and are merely used because they save people the trouble of inventing phrases for themselves." His examples: *ring the changes on, take up the cudgel for, toe the line, ride roughshod over, stand shoulder to shoulder with, play into the hands of, no axe to grind, grist to the mill, fishing in troubled waters, on the order of the day, Achilles' heel, swan song, hotbed.* Diagnostic: "incompatible metaphors are frequently mixed, a sure sign that the writer is not interested in what he is saying" (his example: the hammer and the anvil — "In real life it is always the anvil that breaks the hammer").
2. **Operators or verbal false limbs** — "The keynote is the elimination of simple verbs." Examples: *render inoperative, militate against, make contact with, be subjected to, give rise to, play a leading part (role) in, exhibit a tendency to, serve the purpose of.* Also: passive preferred to active, *by examination of* instead of *by examining*, the *not un-* formation, endings "saved by anticlimax" — *greatly to be desired, cannot be left out of account, brought to a satisfactory conclusion.*
3. **Pretentious diction** — "Words like *phenomenon, element, individual (as noun), objective, categorical, effective, virtual, basic, primary, promote, constitute, exhibit, exploit, utilize, eliminate, liquidate* are used to dress up a simple statement and give an air of scientific impartiality to biased judgements." Plus war-glorifying archaism (*realm, throne, mailed fist, clarion*), needless foreign tags (*cul de sac, ancien regime, deus ex machina, mutatis mutandis, status quo, weltanschauung*), and the rule of thumb: "Bad writers ... are nearly always haunted by the notion that Latin or Greek words are grander than Saxon ones."
4. **Meaningless words** — in art criticism: *romantic, plastic, values, human, dead, sentimental, natural, vitality* — "strictly meaningless, in the sense that they not only do not point to any discoverable object, but are hardly ever expected to do so by the reader." In politics: "The word Fascism has now no meaning except in so far as it signifies 'something not desirable'." Also *democracy, socialism, freedom, patriotic, realistic, justice* — "often used in a consciously dishonest way."

Key mechanism sentences [PRIMARY]: "the writer knows more or less what he wants to say, but an accumulation of stale phrases chokes him like tea leaves blocking a sink." — "You can shirk it by simply throwing your mind open and letting the ready-made phrases come crowding in. They will construct your sentences for you — even think your thoughts for you." — "It [modern writing] consists in gumming together long strips of words which have already been set in order by someone else, and making the results presentable by sheer humbug."

## 2. The Windowpane ("Why I Write", Gangrel, 1946) [PRIMARY — full text verified]

> "And yet it is also true that one can write nothing readable unless one constantly struggles to efface one's own personality. Good prose is like a windowpane."

TEXTUAL VARIANT [CONTRADICTION — recorded, not resolved]: orwell.ru prints "windowpane" (one word); the Collected Essays, Journalism and Letters and several Penguin printings give "window pane" (two words). Same sentence, editions differ.

Operational implications [INFERRED — from the essay's own surrounding text]:
- The metric of good prose is *transparency*: the reader should see the subject, not the writer. It sits in the same paragraph as "efface one's own personality" — windowpane = ego-suppression, not ornament-suppression only.
- It is tied to purpose: "it is invariably where I lacked a political purpose that I wrote lifeless books and was betrayed into purple passages, sentences without meaning, decorative adjectives and humbug" [PRIMARY]. No purpose → opaque glass.
- It is a *struggle*, not a default: "constantly struggles" — plainness is achieved against the drift of ready-made phrases (cf. the tea-leaves image in "Politics").
- His stated trajectory: "of late years I have tried to write less picturesquely and more exactly" [PRIMARY, "Why I Write"] — exactness outranks vividness.

Findings [INFERRED from measurements above]:
- **Length**: medians cluster 13–24 words. Narrative/reportage runs short (A Hanging median 13); argument runs longer (Politics median 24) but stays well under academic norms. Long sentences are almost always *lists* or *catalogues*, not subordination stacks.
- **Rhythm**: long catalogue sentence → short flat sentence. The kill-shot short sentence closes paragraphs ("I had got to shoot the elephant." — "The dead man was a hundred yards away." — "Still, it is a family.").
- **Declarative dominance**: questions are rare in argument (~1–2/1000w) but deployed in concentrated volleys as a set-piece — Marrakech: "Are they really the same flesh as yourself? Do they even have names? Or are they merely a kind of undifferentiated brown stuff, about as individual as bees or coral insects?"; England Your England: "Are there really such things as nations? Are we not forty-six million individuals, all different?" [PRIMARY]
- **Diction**: 57–65% of running words are ≤4 letters (avg word length 4.1–4.7 chars). Narrative essays are the most Anglo-Saxon (Shooting an Elephant 65%); even the language essay, which must quote jargon, stays at 57%. He practises rule (ii).
- **Semicolons**: modest (0–4.2/1000w) — used for balanced pairs ("This man was not dying, he was alive just as we were alive" is comma-spliced; where he wants a hinge he uses the semicolon: "A sahib has got to act like a sahib; he has got to appear resolute"). His workhorse joints are the comma, the dash, and the full stop. Dashes carry asides ("our language — so the argument runs — must inevitably share in the general collapse").
- **Paragraph shape**: narrative essays open scene-first and delay the thesis to a mid-essay epiphany paragraph; argument essays open thesis-first, usually with a concession built in ("Most people ... would admit ... but it is generally assumed"); criticism opens aphorism-first (Dali, Gandhi). Endings snap shut with irony or a flat sentence rather than peroration.

### Famous opening sentences, verbatim [PRIMARY — all verified against full texts]

1. *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (1949): "It was a bright cold day in April, and the clocks were striking thirteen."
2. "A Hanging" (1931): "It was in Burma, a sodden morning of the rains. A sickly light, like yellow tinfoil, was slanting over the high walls into the jail yard."
3. "Shooting an Elephant" (1936): "In Moulmein, in Lower Burma, I was hated by large numbers of people — the only time in my life that I have been important enough for this to happen to me."
4. "Marrakech" (1939): "As the corpse went past the flies left the restaurant table in a cloud and rushed after it, but they came back a few minutes later."
5. *The Lion and the Unicorn* (1941): "As I write, highly civilized human beings are flying overhead, trying to kill me."
6. "Looking Back on the Spanish War" (1943): "It is curious that more vividly than anything that came afterwards in the Spanish war I remember the week of so-called training that we received before being sent to the front..."
7. "Benefit of Clergy" (1944): "Autobiography is only to be trusted when it reveals something disgraceful."
8. "Confessions of a Book Reviewer" (1946): "In a cold but stuffy bed-sitting room littered with cigarette ends and half-empty cups of tea, a man in a moth-eaten dressing-gown sits at a rickety table, trying to find room for his typewriter among the piles of dusty papers that surround it."
9. "Why I Write" (1946): "From a very early age, perhaps the age of five or six, I knew that when I grew up I should be a writer."
10. "Reflections on Gandhi" (1949): "Saints should always be judged guilty until they are proved innocent, but the tests that have to be applied to them are not, of course, the same in all cases."
11. *The Road to Wigan Pier* (1937): "The first sound in the mornings was the clumping of the mill-girls' clogs down the cobbled street."

THE PATTERN [INFERRED from the eleven above]: three opening gambits, no throat-clearing in any of them. (a) **Drop into a concrete scene mid-action**, time/place/sense-data in the first clause, one detail slightly wrong or off-kilter (clocks striking thirteen; flies returning; bombers overhead described as "highly civilized human beings"). (b) **A self-implicating autobiographical fact** stated flatly, often unflattering (hated by large numbers of people; knew I should be a writer). (c) **An aphorism asserted without preamble**, then immediately complicated (Autobiography...; Saints...). In all three, the first sentence already contains the essay's whole moral tension in miniature.

## 4. Signature Moves

**a) Concrete sensory detail, above all SMELL.** [PRIMARY examples; SECONDARY framing]
- Wigan Pier, the Brookers' lodging house: "the smell hit you in the face with a smack."
- Wigan Pier: "It is a kind of duty to see and smell such places now and again, especially smell them, lest you should forget that they exist."
- "Looking Back on the Spanish War": "One of the essential experiences of war is never being able to escape from disgusting smells of human origin"; and "First of all the physical memories, the sounds, the smells and the surfaces of things."
- Wigan Pier, the class chapter: "The words were: *The lower classes smell*. That was what we were taught... For no feeling of like or dislike is quite so fundamental as a physical feeling."
- [SECONDARY] Critics treat smell as his signature sense; John Sutherland devoted an entire book to it, *Orwell's Nose: A Pathological Biography* (Reaktion, 2016). [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED as to Sutherland's exact argument; the book's existence and thesis are well attested.]

**b) Exact prices, wages, and measurements used as argument.** [PRIMARY]
- "Books v. Cigarettes" (1946) is an essay-length sum: he counts his own library ("900 books, at a cost of £165 15s"), amortizes it, and concludes reading costs him ~£25 a year against ~£40 a year on tobacco and alcohol — the working-man's claim that books are too dear is thereby refuted by arithmetic, not assertion.
- Wigan Pier reprints household budgets line by line: "Rent 9 0½ / Clothing Club 3 0 / Coal 2 0 / Gas 1 3 ... Total 1 12 0", then argues from the rows: "Please notice that this budget contains *nothing for fuel*."
- A Hanging: "Each cell measured about ten feet by ten." The dead man is "a hundred yards away." Numbers do emotional work while looking like bookkeeping.

**c) Self-implication; confession of discreditable motives.** [PRIMARY]
- "Benefit of Clergy" (1944), verified verbatim: "Autobiography is only to be trusted when it reveals something disgraceful. A man who gives a good account of himself is probably lying, since any life when viewed from the inside is simply a series of defeats."
- "Politics and the English Language": "Look back through this essay, and for certain you will find that I have again and again committed the very faults I am protesting against."
- "Shooting an Elephant": "And afterwards I was very glad that the coolie had been killed; it put me legally in the right... I often wondered whether any of the others grasped that I had done it solely to avoid looking a fool."
- "Why I Write": "All writers are vain, selfish, and lazy, and at the very bottom of their motives there lies a mystery." And of egoism as motive: "It is humbug to pretend this is not a motive, and a strong one."

**d) Irony and understatement.** [PRIMARY]
- The flies "came back a few minutes later" (Marrakech).
- "We all had a drink together, native and European alike, quite amicably. The dead man was a hundred yards away." (A Hanging)
- The bombers: "Most of them, I have no doubt, are kind-hearted law-abiding men who would never dream of committing murder in private life." (Lion and the Unicorn)
- The chant before execution: "not urgent and fearful like a prayer or a cry for help, but steady, rhythmical, almost like the tolling of a bell." (A Hanging)

**e) Aphoristic reversal — take a received formula and flip one hinge.** [PRIMARY]
- "ALL ANIMALS ARE EQUAL / BUT SOME ANIMALS ARE MORE EQUAL THAN OTHERS" (*Animal Farm*, 1945, verified).
- "Saints should always be judged guilty until they are proved innocent." (Gandhi, 1949)
- "Probably the battle of Waterloo was won on the playing-fields of Eton, but the opening battles of all subsequent wars have been lost there." (England Your England, 1941)
- "when the white man turns tyrant it is his own freedom that he destroys." (Shooting an Elephant, 1936)
- "Four legs good, two legs BETTER!" (*Animal Farm* — the sheep's amended chant, verified)

**f) The list of particulars.** [PRIMARY]
- England Your England: "The clatter of clogs in the Lancashire mill towns, the to-and-fro of the lorries on the Great North Road, the queues outside the Labour Exchanges, the rattle of pin-tables in the Soho pubs, the old maids hiking to Holy Communion through the mists of the autumn morning — all these are not only fragments, but characteristic fragments, of the English scene." [TEXTUAL VARIANT: orwell.ru prints "hiking"; the phrase is very widely quoted, including by the Orwell Foundation and in John Major's 1993 paraphrase, as "biking". Recorded, not resolved — check Davison CW XII.]
- Spanish War: the roll-call — "Manuel Gonzalez, Pedro Aguilar, Ramon Fenellosa, Roque Ballaster, Jaime Domenech, Sebastian Viltron, Ramon Nuvo Bosch."
- Wigan Pier: "slag-heaps, chimneys, piled scrap-iron, foul canals, paths of cindery mud criss-crossed by the prints of clogs."
- Even his prohibitions are lists: the dying-metaphor and pretentious-diction inventories in "Politics" are themselves the move.

**g) Addressing the reader plainly.** [PRIMARY] Second person as working instrument, not decoration: "Look back through this essay..."; "You can shirk it by simply throwing your mind open..."; Wigan Pier: "lest *you* should forget that they exist."

**h) Willingness to say "I do not know" / "nothing very much".** [PRIMARY]
- After the trousers anecdote (Spanish War): "What does this incident demonstrate? Nothing very much, because..."
- "Why I Write": "I cannot say with certainty which of my motives are the strongest, but I know which of them deserve to be followed."
- "Politics": "I should expect to find — this is a guess which I have not sufficient knowledge to verify — that the German, Russian and Italian languages have all deteriorated..."

## 5. Verbal Tics & Habits [PRIMARY — counted across the ten fetched essay texts, ~30,000 words]

- "probably" ×38; "seem/seems/seemed" ×55; "perhaps" ×20 — hedged empiricism is the default narration mode.
- "at any rate" ×16 (e.g. "four great motives for writing, at any rate for writing prose" — the hedge is built into his most-quoted taxonomy).
- "of course" ×16 (concessive, reader-including).
- "a sort of" ×11; "more or less" ×10; "no doubt" ×6; "so far as" ×5; "presumably" ×2 ("a man presumably carrying a message to an officer" — hedging even inside an anecdote).
- "It is curious" ×2, both load-bearing: it opens "Looking Back on the Spanish War" and introduces the moral center of "A Hanging" ("It is curious, but till that moment I had never realized what it means to destroy a healthy, conscious man.").
- **Rejection of exclamation and gush**: exclamation marks effectively absent from his own narration (0–1 per 1000 words; the A Hanging spike is all quoted chant/dialogue). No intensifier stacking; "very" is rare; superlatives almost always ironized ("extraordinarily funny" — of a joke told minutes after a hanging).

## 6. Humour [PRIMARY examples; INFERRED classification]

- **Black comedy embedded in horror**: the dog in "A Hanging" — "a dreadful thing had happened — a dog, come goodness knows whence, had appeared in the yard ... wild with glee at finding so many human beings together ... jumping up tried to lick his face" — comedy staged inside an execution, and the laughter afterwards ("At that moment Francis's anecdote seemed extraordinarily funny") indicts everyone laughing, narrator included.
- **Deadpan reportage**: the Marrakech flies; the book reviewer's "moth-eaten dressing-gown" and wastepaper basket "already overflowing".
- **Satiric inversion sustained at length**: *Animal Farm* entire; "Four legs good, two legs BETTER!"; the pigs in Mr Jones's clothes.
- **Self-deflation**: "my prosaic English name made a sort of comic interlude among the resounding Spanish ones" (Spanish War); "I am a poor shot and unlikely to hit a running man at a hundred yards."
- NOT whimsy, NOT self-congratulation, almost never a joke for its own sake: the laugh always carries a moral payload. [INFERRED from the corpus above]

## 7. Certainty Style: Blunt on Morals, Hedged on Facts [PRIMARY — both documented]

Blunt moral assertion, no hedge:
- "Political language ... is designed to make lies sound truthful and murder respectable, and to give an appearance of solidity to pure wind." (Politics, 1946)
- "In our time, political speech and writing are largely the defence of the indefensible." (Politics, 1946)
- "the mystery, the unspeakable wrongness, of cutting a life short when it is in full tide." (A Hanging, 1931)
- "All colonial empires are in reality founded upon that fact." (Marrakech, 1939)

Scrupulous factual hedging, often inside the same essay:
- "this is a guess which I have not sufficient knowledge to verify" (Politics).
- "Early in life I have noticed that no event is ever correctly reported in a newspaper, but in Spain, for the first time, I saw newspaper reports which did not bear any relation to the facts" — the empirical claim is autobiographical and bounded ("I saw"), before the moral alarm: "This kind of thing is frightening to me, because it often gives me the feeling that the very concept of objective truth is fading out of the world." (Spanish War, 1943)
- "Probably the battle of Waterloo was won..."; "For all one knows that demon is simply the same instinct that makes a baby squall for attention." (Why I Write)

Rule of thumb [INFERRED]: he asserts *values* in the indicative without qualification, and *facts* with a visible evidence-chain (I saw / I counted / so far as I can discover / a guess I cannot verify). The confidence gradient is the reverse of most writers'.

## 9. Misattribution Blacklist — NOT Orwell, or unsourced

1. **"In a time of universal deceit, telling the truth is a revolutionary act."** No trace in his works. Quote Investigator: earliest known attribution 1982 (V. G. Venturini, *Partners in Ecocide*, as an unsourced epigraph); QI concludes it "began as a proposed summary of Orwell's position and was later incorrectly converted into a quotation." [SECONDARY — Quote Investigator]
2. **"The further a society drifts from truth, the more it will hate those who speak it."** Written by Selwyn Duke, 2009 ("Stopping Truth at the Border"); Snopes found no earlier instance and Duke has publicly fought the misattribution. [SECONDARY — Snopes]
3. **"People sleep peaceably in their beds at night only because rough men stand ready to do violence on their behalf."** Quote Investigator: earliest matching text is film critic Richard Grenier (Washington Times, 1993), without quotation marks — i.e., Grenier's paraphrase of Orwell's actual sentences (#28 and #29 above). Also wrongly given to Churchill. [SECONDARY — Quote Investigator]
4. **"Journalism is printing what someone else does not want printed: everything else is public relations."** No Orwell source. The saying family predates him without his name — Quote Investigator traces "news is something someone does not want you to print; all the rest is advertising" to 1918 (*The Fourth Estate*), later attached to Hearst, then to Orwell. [SECONDARY — Quote Investigator]
5. **Caution class** [INFERRED]: lines spoken by *characters* (O'Brien's boot, #5) are routinely quoted as Orwell's own opinion; and orwell.ru/Gutenberg transcriptions contain typos — always check a printed Davison text before treating wording as canonical.

## Recorded Contradictions (unharmonized)

- "windowpane" (orwell.ru "Why I Write") vs "window pane" (CEJL/Penguin printings). [textual]
- "old maids **hiking** to Holy Communion" (orwell.ru "England Your England") vs "old maids **biking** to Holy Communion" (as commonly printed and quoted, incl. Orwell Foundation excerpts). [textual — check Davison, *Complete Works* XII]
