# Dimension 6 — Timeline: George Orwell (Eric Arthur Blair, 1903–1950) + Posthumous Legacy to 2026

Research date: 2026-07-20. Language: English.

**Method and labels.** [PRIMARY] = Orwell's own words in a named work/letter/diary. [SECONDARY] = biographer/critic/press claim (named). [INFERRED] = my inference (source stated). [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED] = drawn from model knowledge of the standard biographical record (Crick 1980; Shelden 1991; Taylor 2003/2023; Bowker 2003; Davison's *Complete Works* 1998 chronology) but not re-verified online in this session. Items marked **(v)** were verified this session against a fetched page or a search result from a serious source.

**Misquotation warning.** Orwell is among the most misquoted writers in English. The following circulate constantly and are **apocryphal/unsourced — never use them**: "In a time of universal deceit, telling the truth is a revolutionary act"; "The further a society drifts from truth, the more it will hate those who speak it"; "People sleep peaceably in their beds at night only because rough men stand ready to do violence on their behalf." None appears in Davison's 20-volume *Complete Works*. Every quotation below is tied to a named work and year.

## Part A — Life, 1903–1950

Blanket sourcing for the table: [SECONDARY — biographical consensus of Crick 1980, Taylor 2003/2023, Davison 1998 chronology; EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED except rows marked (v)]. Quotations labeled individually.

| Year(s) | Event | How it shaped his thinking (for formative events) |
|---|---|---|
| 25 Jun 1903 | Born Eric Arthur Blair at Motihari, Bengal Presidency (now Bihar), British India; father Richard W. Blair, sub-deputy agent in the Opium Department; mother Ida Limouzin | Born inside the imperial machine — his father literally administered the opium trade. Later self-description: "lower-upper-middle class" (*The Road to Wigan Pier*, 1937) [PRIMARY] — a lifelong sensitivity to fine class gradations. |
| 1904 | Mother takes Eric and sister Marjorie to England; settles at Henley-on-Thames | Raised in genteel-shabby England while father stays in India; absent-father childhood. |
| 1911–1916 | St Cyprian's prep school, Eastbourne, on reduced fees | Formative humiliation: the reduced-fee boy among the rich. His posthumously published account "Such, Such Were the Joys" (written late 1940s; publ. *Partisan Review* 1952) [PRIMARY] describes beatings, snobbery, and induced guilt — his first model of an arbitrary, total power system. **Contradiction recorded below** (§ Contradictions #1). |
| 1917–Dec 1921 | One term at Wellington, then King's Scholar at Eton (May 1917); taught some French by Aldous Huxley; idles academically | Learned the protective coloration of the elite without joining it; left with no university path — set up the Burma decision. |
| Oct 1922–1927 | Indian Imperial Police, Burma: Mandalay training, then postings incl. Twante, Syriam, Insein, Moulmein, Katha | Five years as the empire's armed enforcer. "I was in the Indian Police five years, and by the end of that time I hated the imperialism I was serving with a bitterness which I probably cannot make clear" (*The Road to Wigan Pier*, 1937) [PRIMARY]. Source of "A Hanging" (1931), "Shooting an Elephant" (1936), *Burmese Days* (1934). Taught him what it feels like to be the face of illegitimate power — the guilt that drove everything after. |
| 1927–Jan 1928 | Home on leave Aug 1927; resigns from the police (effective 1 Jan 1928) against family wishes; begins deliberate descent into poverty (East End tramping) | Self-imposed penance: "I felt that I had got to escape not merely from imperialism but from every form of man's dominion over man" (*Wigan Pier*) [PRIMARY]. Method established: go there yourself, then write. |
| Spring 1928–Dec 1929 | Paris, rue du Pot de Fer: first published journalism (first professional article in *Monde*, Oct 1928); pneumonia at Hôpital Cochin Feb 1929 (→ essay "How the Poor Die", 1946); works as *plongeur* (dishwasher) | Poverty stopped being a concept and became data. The hospital ward and the hotel scullery gave him a bottom-up view of institutions. |
| 1930–1932 | London tramping, hop-picking in Kent (Sep 1931); "The Spike" and "A Hanging" in *Adelphi* (1931); teaching at The Hawthorns school, Hayes (1932–33) | — |
| 9 Jan 1933 | *Down and Out in Paris and London* (Gollancz) — pseudonym "George Orwell" adopted (rejected alternatives incl. P. S. Burton, Kenneth Miles, H. Lewis Allways) | The persona is born: plain-spoken English witness, named for an English river. |
| 1934–1936 | *Burmese Days* (US Oct 1934; UK 1935); *A Clergyman's Daughter* (1935); *Keep the Aspidistra Flying* (1936); part-time assistant at Booklovers' Corner bookshop, Hampstead (Oct 1934–Jan 1936) | Apprentice novels; he later largely disowned *Clergyman's Daughter* and *Aspidistra* [SECONDARY: Davison apparatus — he refused reprints in his lifetime]. |
| Jan–Mar 1936 | Two months in Wigan, Barnsley, Sheffield among miners and the unemployed, commissioned by Gollancz | Descent #2, this time into the English working class. Produced *The Road to Wigan Pier* (Mar 1937) — Part I reportage, Part II a scorching attack on middle-class socialists, so awkward that Gollancz added a distancing foreword to his own Left Book Club edition [SECONDARY: Crick]. Fixed his stance: socialist, but the movement's fiercest internal critic. |
| Apr–Jun 1936 | Moves to The Stores, Wallington, Hertfordshire (keeps goats, hens, village shop); marries Eileen O'Shaughnessy, 9 Jun 1936 | The domestic-rural counterweight to politics — gardens, animals, "the surface of the earth" (*Why I Write*, 1946) [PRIMARY]. Wallington's animals and pond furnished *Animal Farm*'s setting. |
| Dec 1936–Jun 1937 | Spain: joins POUM militia via ILP; Aragon front; Barcelona May Days (3–8 May 1937) fighting on the POUM side; **20 May 1937 shot through the throat by a sniper near Huesca**; POUM suppressed as "Trotskyist-fascist"; he and Eileen flee to France 23 Jun 1937 with police hunting them | THE hinge of his life. He watched the Communist press fabricate reality about events he had personally witnessed: "I saw great battles reported where there had been no fighting… I saw newspapers in London retailing these lies… I saw, in fact, history being written not in terms of what happened but of what ought to have happened according to various 'party lines'" ("Looking Back on the Spanish War", 1943) [PRIMARY]. Direct germ of the Ministry of Truth. "Every line of serious work that I have written since 1936 has been written, directly or indirectly, against totalitarianism and for democratic socialism, as I understand it" ("Why I Write", *Gangrel*, 1946) [PRIMARY]. |
| Apr 1938 | *Homage to Catalonia* (Secker & Warburg, after Gollancz refused it unseen); commercial failure — roughly 600–700 of 1,500 copies sold by his death [SECONDARY: Warburg's account via Crick/Davison] | Learned the price of telling the left truths about the left; cemented his alliance with the small independent publisher Warburg. |
| 1938–1939 | Tubercular lesion; Preston Hall sanatorium; winter in Marrakech funded by an anonymous £300 loan (novelist L. H. Myers) → essay "Marrakech" (1939); *Coming Up for Air* (Jun 1939); father dies Jun 1939 | Illness becomes a permanent companion; *Coming Up for Air* rehearses the nostalgia-vs-coming-war theme. |
| 1940–1941 | Rejected for army service (lungs); moves to London; Home Guard sergeant; *Inside the Whale* (1940); *The Lion and the Unicorn: Socialism and the English Genius* (Feb 1941) — "England is the most class-ridden country under the sun" but also his case for a patriotic English revolution [PRIMARY] | Wartime London fused his socialism with English patriotism — an unusual combination that defines his brand of decency politics. |
| Aug 1941–Nov 1943 | BBC Eastern Service (India section), Talks producer: 200+ wartime newsletters and cultural programmes beamed to India | Two years producing what he privately regarded as propaganda. Resignation letter (Sep 1943): "I am wasting my own time and the public money on doing work that produces no result" [PRIMARY, letter in Davison CW]. BBC canteen bureaucracy is the standard story behind "Room 101" (a meeting room at 55 Portland Place) [SECONDARY, commonly repeated; Davison notes the association]. Gave him inside knowledge of institutional truth-management — raw material for the Ministry of Truth. |
| Nov 1943–1945 | Literary editor of *Tribune*; "As I Please" column (from 3 Dec 1943; ~80 columns through 1947) | His freest, most personal journalism — politics, toads, murder, language, all in one voice. |
| Nov 1943–Feb 1944 | Writes *Animal Farm* (with Eileen's nightly input — see Contradictions #3) | — |
| 1944 | Rejected by Gollancz, Nicholson & Watson, Cape (Cape withdrew after advice from a Ministry of Information official, later identified as Soviet agent Peter Smollett/Smolka [SECONDARY: Davison; Taylor]), and Faber — T. S. Eliot's letter, 13 Jul 1944: the book's viewpoint "is not the right point of view from which to criticise the political situation" [SECONDARY: Eliot letter, published, widely reproduced]. One US house reportedly said it was "impossible to sell animal stories in the USA" [PRIMARY: Orwell's own account, "The Freedom of the Press" preface draft]. Adopts son Richard Horatio Blair (b. 14 May 1944); flat destroyed by V-1 (Jun 1944), *Animal Farm* typescript dug from rubble | The suppression experience produced the unused preface "The Freedom of the Press" — source of the true quote: "If liberty means anything at all, it means the right to tell people what they do not want to hear" [PRIMARY: proposed preface to *Animal Farm*, written 1945, first publ. TLS 1972] **(v — inscription source confirmed via BBC statue documentation)**. |
| Feb–May 1945 | War correspondent (*Observer*/*Manchester Evening News*) in Paris and Cologne; **Eileen dies 29 Mar 1945**, aged 39, under anaesthetic for a hysterectomy, while he is abroad | Widowed with an infant son; guilt and grief shadow the last five years. Eileen's erasure/recovery is the core of Funder's *Wifedom* (2023) — see Contradictions #3. |
| 17 Aug 1945 | *Animal Farm* published (Secker & Warburg); instant success; US edition Harcourt Brace Aug 1946, Book-of-the-Month Club selection → first real money and international fame | — |
| Oct 1945 | "You and the Atom Bomb" (*Tribune*, 19 Oct 1945) — uses and popularizes the phrase "cold war" [PRIMARY] | — |
| 1946 | Peak essay year: "Politics and the English Language" (*Horizon*, Apr 1946), "Why I Write" (*Gangrel*), "The Prevention of Literature", "Decline of the English Murder", "Some Thoughts on the Common Toad", "Politics vs. Literature" [PRIMARY works] | Codified his method: clear language as a political act. |
| May 1946–1948 | Moves to Barnhill, Isle of Jura (sister Marjorie dies May 1946; sister Avril keeps house); drafts *Nineteen Eighty-Four* (working title "The Last Man in Europe"); Aug 1947 nearly drowns with son and nephews in the Corryvreckan whirlpool; **Dec 1947 TB diagnosed** — Hairmyres Hospital; experimental streptomycin obtained from the US via David Astor fails with severe toxic reaction [SECONDARY: Crick; the remaining drug was passed to two other patients who recovered]; back on Jura Jul 1948; types the final MS himself, finishing 4 Dec 1948 | The race between the book and the disease. The retyping on Jura in bed is standard lore; biographers agree the effort wrecked him, but deny the myth that the book's bleakness is merely illness [SECONDARY: Crick; Lynskey 2019 explicitly rebuts "he wrote it because he was dying"]. |
| Jan–Jun 1949 | Cranham sanatorium, Gloucestershire. May 1949: gives Celia Kirwan of the Foreign Office's Information Research Department a list of ~38 writers/public figures he considered unreliable as anti-communist propagandists ("Orwell's list") — see Contradictions #2. **8 Jun 1949**: *Nineteen Eighty-Four* published (Secker & Warburg; US 13 Jun, Harcourt Brace); immediate transatlantic sensation | Against readings of the novel as anti-socialist, he dictated a press statement (Jul 1949, via Warburg/Life/NYT Book Review): "The moral to be drawn from this dangerous nightmare situation is a simple one: Don't let it happen. It depends on you." [PRIMARY: statement in Davison CW XX] |
| Sep 1949–1950 | University College Hospital, London; **13 Oct 1949 marries Sonia Brownell** in his hospital room; plans a Swiss sanatorium trip; **dies 21 Jan 1950**, aged 46, of a massive pulmonary hemorrhage; buried 26 Jan at All Saints', Sutton Courtenay, Oxfordshire (grave arranged by David Astor), headstone reading only "Here Lies Eric Arthur Blair" — no "George Orwell" | Died at his fame's ignition point; never saw the Cold War weaponization of his books. Will asked that no biography be written [SECONDARY: Crick, who discusses overriding it]. |

## Contradictions (recorded, not harmonized)

1. **Was St Cyprian's really that bad?** Orwell's "Such, Such Were the Joys" [PRIMARY] paints systematic cruelty and snobbery. But Jacintha Buddicom (*Eric & Us*, 1974) [SECONDARY] insisted the boy she knew was "a specially happy child", and Cyril Connolly's *Enemies of Promise* (1938) [SECONDARY] gives a far more ambivalent, partly fond account of the same school. Crick [SECONDARY] treats the essay as shaped literary art, not deposition. Unresolved: memoir vs. contemporaries.
2. **"Orwell's list" (May 1949).** Fact: he sent Celia Kirwan of the IRD a notebook-derived list of ~38 people he judged crypto-communists or fellow-travellers, unsuitable for writing British anti-communist propaganda (full list published 2003; Guardian coverage). Interpretations clash head-on: critics (e.g., Alexander Cockburn — who called him a "snitch") read it as betrayal and blacklisting; defenders (Christopher Hitchens, Timothy Garton Ash) note it was private advice about propaganda commissions, not denunciation, from a dying democratic socialist who had fought actual Stalinist persecution in Spain [SECONDARY both sides]. Record: both readings persist in the literature.
3. **Eileen's role and Orwell's character — Funder vs. the biographers.** Anna Funder's *Wifedom* (2023) [SECONDARY] argues Eileen O'Shaughnessy was written out of Orwell's life and books (including a shaping role in *Animal Farm*'s comic form, which several biographers do accept) and portrays Orwell as exploitative and at times predatory toward women. Reviewers including D. J. Taylor and other Orwell scholars [SECONDARY] countered that Funder selectively reads evidence and blends fiction with fact ("counter-fictions") in ways that mislead. Unresolved and actively contested as of 2023–26.
4. **"Shooting an Elephant" — memoir or fiction?** Crick [SECONDARY] doubted the event's literal truth; Shelden and later researchers [SECONDARY] found circumstantial support (colleagues' recollections; records of an elephant shot by a Blair-era officer). Davison prints it as an essay while noting the debate. Standing caution: Orwell's first-person "documentary" pieces are shaped artifacts.
5. **CIA and the 1954 *Animal Farm* film.** The covert funding is established (Saunders 1999) [SECONDARY], but colorful specifics — E. Howard Hunt's role, Sonia's "Clark Gable" request — rest on memoir-grade sources and vary between tellings. Fact vs. anecdote boundary is blurry here.
6. **Minor active discrepancy (2026):** Publishing Perspectives' headline names the 2026 Political Writing winner "Kate Bartlett" while the body text says "Karen Bartlett" — first name recorded both ways pending verification.

## Where the full texts live today (verified this session)

- **Project Gutenberg Australia** — http://gutenberg.net.au/pages/orwell.html **(v)**: full novels and documentary books plus a "Fifty Orwell Essays" compilation and a cross-index of which essays appeared in which collections (*Inside the Whale* 1940 through CEJL 1968). Public domain under Australian law; plain-HTTP site (use curl).
- **The Orwell Foundation** — orwellfoundation.com: hosts a curated online library of essays, diaries and letters by permission of the Orwell Estate, and runs the Orwell Prizes, Youth Prize and Orwell Festival [library composition EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED — site blocked non-browser fetching this session; prize/news pages (v)].
- **orwell.ru** — https://orwell.ru/library/ **(v gateway)**: long-running (since 1999) bilingual English/Russian library of novels, essays, articles and reviews.
- **Wikisource (English)** — https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Author:George_Orwell **(v)**: mostly a bibliography/index; can only host what is US-public-domain (pre-1931 items, e.g. the 1914 poem "Awake! Young Men of England"); explicitly notes *Animal Farm* is US-copyrighted until 2041.
- **UCL Orwell Archive** (physical) **(v via UCL News 2025)**: the foremost Orwell archive — diaries, correspondence, notebooks, proofs; UNESCO Memory of the World.
- Print bedrock: **Peter Davison, ed., *The Complete Works of George Orwell***, 20 vols., 1998 (+ *The Lost Orwell*, 2006) — the citation standard.
