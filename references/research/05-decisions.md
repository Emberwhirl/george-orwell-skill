# Dimension 5 — Decisions & Actions: George Orwell (Eric Arthur Blair, 1903-1950)

**Method note.** Every item is tagged: [PRIMARY] = Orwell's own words in a named work/letter/diary (quotes checked against full texts at Project Gutenberg Australia or orwell.ru where marked "verified against full text"); [SECONDARY] = biographer/critic claim, named; [INFERRED] = my inference, with basis; [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED] = from model knowledge of the Orwell literature, not re-verified this session. Contradictions between sources are recorded, not harmonized. Orwell is heavily misquoted; nothing below comes from quote-aggregator sites.

**His own frame for the whole record.** "The Spanish war and other events in 1936-37 turned the scale and thereafter I knew where I stood. Every line of serious work that I have written since 1936 has been written, directly or indirectly, against totalitarianism and for democratic socialism, as I understand it." [PRIMARY: "Why I Write," 1946 — verified against full text, orwell.ru] The decisions below are the deeds that statement summarizes — and the places where the deeds ran ahead of, or against, the words.

## 1. 1922: Eton to the Indian Imperial Police; 1927: resigning while on leave

- **Context.** King's Scholar at Eton, but idled academically; a university scholarship was judged unlikely and his father favored a career. Family had Anglo-Indian roots; his grandmother lived at Moulmein, Burma. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Jacintha Buddicom, *Eric & Us* 1974, says his crammer thought a scholarship improbable]
- **The choice.** Sat the India Office exams, chose the Indian Imperial Police, and requested posting to Burma. Served 1922-27 in half a dozen posts including Katha and Moulmein.
- **Reversal.** "When I came home on leave in 1927 I was already half determined to throw up my job, and one sniff of English air decided me. I was not going back to be a part of that evil despotism." [PRIMARY: *The Road to Wigan Pier*, 1937, Part 2 — verified against full text, Gutenberg Australia]
- **His stated reasoning (retrospective).** "I had already made up my mind that imperialism was an evil thing and the sooner I chucked up my job and got out of it the better. Theoretically — and secretly, of course — I was all for the Burmese and all against their oppressors, the British." [PRIMARY: "Shooting an Elephant," 1936 — verified against full text, orwell.ru]
- And: "Innumerable remembered faces — faces of prisoners in the dock, of men waiting in the condemned cells, of subordinates I had bullied and aged peasants I had snubbed, of servants and coolies I had hit with my fist in moments of rage... haunted me intolerably. I was conscious of an immense weight of guilt that I had got to expiate." [PRIMARY: *Wigan Pier*, Part 2 (ch. IX in standard editions) — verified against full text]
- **Cost.** Resigned a secure imperial career (salary with allowances roughly £650-700/year at the end) for no income and no trade. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980, figure approximate]
- **Caveat.** All the "reasoning" above was written 9-14 years after the fact by a professional writer shaping his own myth; no contemporaneous 1927 statement of motive survives. [INFERRED from the documentary record's silence; Crick makes the same point]
- **Heuristic revealed.** When you conclude the institution you serve is evil, exit — and treat the years served as a debt to be repaid, not merely a mistake to be forgotten.

## 2. 1928-31: deliberate destitution — tramping, plongeur work, hop-picking

- **The choice.** Repeated self-immersion in poverty: dishwashing (plongeur) in Paris hotels (1929), tramping and casual-ward "spikes" in England under the alias P.S. Burton, hop-picking in Kent (1931, recorded in his Hop-Picking Diary). Product: *Down and Out in Paris and London* (1933) and early essays ("The Spike," "Hop-Picking").
- **His stated reasoning.** "I felt that I had got to escape not merely from imperialism but from every form of man's dominion over man. I wanted to submerge myself, to get right down among the oppressed, to be one of them and on their side against their tyrants." And: "At that time failure seemed to me to be the only virtue. Every suspicion of self-advancement... seemed to me spiritually ugly, a species of bullying." [PRIMARY: *Wigan Pier*, Part 2 — verified against full text]
- Also epistemic: "But I knew nothing about working-class conditions. I had read the unemployment figures but I had no notion of what they implied." [PRIMARY: same source]
- **What it reveals.** Immersion as epistemology: he did not trust statistics or received opinion until he had physically verified conditions with his own body. He repeats the method all his life — Wigan 1936, Spain 1936-37, following Londoners into Tube shelters in the Blitz. [INFERRED from the pattern of Wigan Pier, Homage, wartime diaries]
- **Honesty caveat, his own.** "I think I can say that I have exaggerated nothing except in so far as all writers exaggerate by selecting. I did not feel that I had to describe events in the exact order in which they happened." [PRIMARY: Introduction to the French edition of *Down and Out* (*La Vache enragée*, Gallimard, 1935); reproduced in Davison's note, orwell.ru] — i.e., he admitted the "documentary" was arranged. Also the safety-net caveat: he could and did retreat to family houses between plunges; the destitution was elective. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Bowker 2003]
- **Heuristic revealed.** Go and see for yourself; distrust any claim about how people live that you have not tested against direct experience — but disclose your method's limits.

## 3. 1932-33: the pen name "George Orwell"

- **The choice.** For *Down and Out* he asked his agent Leonard Moore to publish pseudonymously: "A name I always use when tramping etc. is P. S. Burton but if you don't think this sounds a probable kind of name, what about Kenneth Miles, George Orwell, H. Lewis Allways. I rather favour George Orwell." [PRIMARY: letter to Leonard Moore, November 1932, in Davison, *Complete Works*; quoted in multiple secondary sources this session]
- **Reasons given/attributed.** Sparing his family embarrassment over the tramping book; he also disliked "Eric." The name: an English patron saint's plain first name plus the River Orwell in Suffolk, near Southwold. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Shelden 1991] A later remark to Rayner Heppenstall that an enemy who knows your real name can "work some kind of magic on it" is often cited. [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED]
- **Note.** He never legally changed his name; friends from different eras called him Eric or George, and he signed some late documents Eric Blair (the will of 18 January 1950 is signed Eric Blair). [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Orwell Society]
- **Heuristic revealed.** Separate the writing self from the private self; choose plainness (a river, a common first name) over cleverness.

## 4. 1936-37: the Wigan Pier commission and the Gollancz foreword

- **Context.** Victor Gollancz commissioned a book on unemployment and conditions in the industrial North; Orwell spent roughly February-March 1936 in Wigan, Barnsley, Sheffield — lodging over the tripe shop, going down Crippen's mine. The often-repeated £500 advance is not securely documented; biographers repeat it, Crick and Davison treat it with caution. [SECONDARY — CONTESTED: £500 figure widely cited (e.g., Taylor 2003) but questioned in Davison's textual note]
- **The conflict.** Part 1 was reportage; Part 2 attacked the middle-class socialist intelligentsia itself. Gollancz "first tried to persuade Orwell's agent to allow the Left Book Club edition to consist solely of the descriptive first half of the book. When this was refused, Gollancz wrote an introduction" publicly dissenting from Part 2 — a publisher distancing himself from his own book. The Left Book Club printed ~44,000 copies (figures vary ~40,000-47,000). [SECONDARY: Davison, "Note on The Road to Wigan Pier," Orwell Foundation; Wikipedia summary of same]
- **Complication (record, don't harmonize).** Orwell did not fight the foreword itself: back from Spain he wrote thanking Gollancz, saying he liked the introduction very much though he could have answered its criticisms. A Part-1-only supplementary LBC edition did later appear. [SECONDARY: Davison note; Rooke Books/Peter Harrington bibliographic descriptions]
- **Heuristic revealed.** Keep the offensive half of the truth in the book even when the buyer flinches — but accept open, signed disagreement printed alongside; answered speech, not deletion, is the currency.

## 5. Dec 1936-June 1937: Spain — militia, May Days, wound, flight

- **The choice.** Married Eileen O'Shaughnessy 9 June 1936; left for Spain about six months later (Barcelona, late December 1936). "I had come to Spain with some notion of writing newspaper articles, but I had joined the militia almost immediately, because at that time and in that atmosphere it seemed the only conceivable thing to do." [PRIMARY: *Homage to Catalonia*, 1938, ch. 1 — verified against full text]
- He landed in the POUM militia by organizational accident — he carried Independent Labour Party credentials, not Communist ones. Wounded, he wrote to Cyril Connolly (8 June 1937): "I have seen wonderful things and at last really believe in Socialism, which I never did before." [PRIMARY: letter to Connolly, 8 June 1937, *Complete Works*; quoted in ILP and Spartacus materials this session]
- **The transfer question.** Before the May Days he planned to move to the Communist-run International Column to reach Madrid: "I told everyone that when we went on leave I should, if possible, exchange into the International Column, which meant putting myself under Communist control." After witnessing the Barcelona street fighting (May 1937) he refused the renewed invitation: "I had to tell him that after this affair I could not join any Communist-controlled unit. Sooner or later it might mean being used against the Spanish working class." [PRIMARY: *Homage to Catalonia* — both verified against full text] Note the sequence: the evidence changed his allegiance within days, publicly, at cost to his one route to the "real" front. [INFERRED from the two passages]
- **How he decided which side to hold in the May Days confusion.** "When I see an actual flesh-and-blood worker in conflict with his natural enemy, the policeman, I do not have to ask myself which side I am on." [PRIMARY: *Homage*, on taking his post during the Barcelona fighting — verified against full text] Decision by concrete instance, not by party line. [INFERRED]
- **The wound.** Shot through the throat by a sniper near Huesca, 20 May 1937. "Nobody I met at this time... failed to assure me that a man who is hit through the neck and survives it is the luckiest creature alive. I could not help thinking that it would be even luckier not to be hit at all." [PRIMARY: *Homage* — verified against full text]
- **The purge and flight.** June 1937: POUM suppressed, comrades jailed. Bob Smillie, 22, died in a Valencia prison — Orwell recorded the uncertainty rather than the convenient version: "Of course I assumed at once that Smillie had been shot... but I have since thought that I may have been wrong... So perhaps the appendicitis story was true." Still: "Smillie's death is not a thing I can easily forgive." [PRIMARY: *Homage* — verified against full text]
- While himself a wanted man sleeping in ruins, he and Eileen visited Georges Kopp in jail, and Orwell personally talked his way into the War Department — voice half-gone from the throat wound — to retrieve the seized letter that might establish Kopp's bona fides: "time slipping away and a life perhaps in the balance." [PRIMARY: *Homage* — verified against full text] He, Eileen, McNair and Cottman escaped by train into France on 23 June 1937, posing as tourists. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Taylor 2003]
- **Heuristics revealed.** (a) Act first, theorize later ("the only conceivable thing to do"). (b) Change sides against your own convenience the moment the evidence demands it. (c) Even in flight, spend personal risk on individual loyalty (Kopp). (d) Report your own uncertainty (Smillie) even when the atrocity version serves your cause.

## 6. 1937-38: publishing the truth about Barcelona against the left press

- **The refusals.** Kingsley Martin's *New Statesman* rejected his eyewitness reporting ("Eyewitness in Barcelona" ultimately ran in the tiny ILP-adjacent *Controversy*; "Spilling the Spanish Beans" in *New English Weekly*, July-September 1937 [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED on exact venues/dates]); as consolation Martin commissioned a review of Borkenau's *The Spanish Cockpit*, then rejected that too on the stated grounds that "it is very uncompromisingly said and implies that our Spanish correspondents are all wrong." [SECONDARY: New Statesman's own retrospective, 2013/2021; Crick 1980]
- Gollancz declined *Homage to Catalonia* on political grounds — by the standard account before reading it. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Taylor 2003] Fredric Warburg (Secker & Warburg) took it.
- **The cost.** Ostracism from the dominant left-intellectual organ of his country; a book that failed commercially. Print/sales figures conflict: 1,500 copies printed; "only sold 800" with the rest unsold until after his death [SECONDARY: Barcelona Metropolitan 75th-anniversary feature]; "fewer than 1,000 copies sold by his death in 1950" [SECONDARY: other accounts this session]; ~900 is the commonly cited round figure [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED]. No second UK edition until 1951. — CONTRADICTION RECORDED: 800 vs <1,000 vs ~900; all agree the run never sold out in his lifetime.
- **The grudge.** Years later, lunching with Malcolm Muggeridge, Orwell asked to swap seats so he would not have to look at Kingsley Martin's "corrupt face." [SECONDARY: Muggeridge's recollection, retold in Crick 1980 and D.J. Taylor 2003]
- **The epistemic signature.** He printed the warning against himself: "I believe that on such an issue as this no one is or can be completely truthful... beware of my partisanship, my mistakes of fact, and the distortion inevitably caused by my having seen only one corner of events. And beware of exactly the same things when you read any other book on this period of the Spanish war." [PRIMARY: *Homage*, final chapter — verified against full text]
- **Heuristic revealed.** Publish what you saw even when your own side's gatekeepers refuse it and the market punishes it; and stamp the account with its own error bars.

## 7. 1939-40: the overnight switch to supporting the war

- **Context.** Through 1937-39 he was anti-war, close to ILP positions: "For several years the coming war was a nightmare to me, and at times I even made speeches and wrote pamphlets against it." [PRIMARY: "My Country Right or Left," 1940 — verified against full text, orwell.ru]
- **The switch, as he told it.** "The night before the Russo-German pact was announced I dreamed that the war had started... It taught me two things, first, that I should be simply relieved when the long-dreaded war started, secondly, that I was patriotic at heart, would not sabotage or act against my own side, would support the war, would fight in it if possible." [PRIMARY: same essay — verified against full text]
- **Actions.** Tried repeatedly to enlist and to get war work; rejected as medically unfit (tubercular lungs). Served instead as a sergeant in the Home Guard (C Company, St John's Wood) and wrote its tactics up seriously. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Taylor 2003; specific unit designation EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED]
- **What it cost.** His ILP/pacifist alliances; he then attacked pacifists in print with the same vehemence he had lately shared. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; INFERRED: the dream framing converts an intellectual reversal into an involuntary discovery — a rhetorical device that spared him a recantation]
- **Heuristic revealed.** Treat your gut reactions as data about your real beliefs; when instinct and doctrine split, follow the instinct and say so publicly.

## 8. 1941-43: taking the BBC propaganda job, then resigning

- **The choice.** August 1941: joined the BBC Eastern Service producing cultural/news programming aimed at India — wartime propaganda by his own description. His wartime diary (14 March 1942) contains the line "All propaganda is lies, even when one is telling the truth." [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED exact date/wording; the diary is published by the Orwell Foundation/Davison]
- **The resignation.** Letter to L.F. Rushbrook Williams, 24 September 1943: "I am tendering my resignation because for some time past I have been conscious that I was wasting my own time and the public money on doing work that produces no result... I have never been compelled to say on the air anything that I would not have said as a private individual"; he wished to return to "my normal work of writing and journalism." [PRIMARY: resignation letter, *Complete Works*; quoted in search sources this session]
- **His verdict.** "I have left the BBC after two wasted years" — letter to Philip Rahv, 9 December 1943. [PRIMARY: quoted by Peter Davison, Orwell Society]
- **CONTRADICTION RECORDED.** Davison, editor of the *Complete Works* (who titled the 1943 volume *Two Wasted Years*), disputes Orwell's own verdict: "I cannot agree that his months at the BBC were 'two wasted years'. Indeed, given the circumstances his achievements were formidable." [SECONDARY: Davison, Orwell Society essay] The BBC canteen and Room 101 at Broadcasting House fed *Nineteen Eighty-Four*'s Ministry of Truth. [SECONDARY: commonplace in Crick, Taylor; Room 101 attribution is tradition more than document]
- **Heuristic revealed.** You may serve a machine you distrust in an emergency, but audit the output ("produces no result") and exit when the ledger fails; also — insist on the one red line (never say what you wouldn't say as a private individual).

## 9. 1943-45: Animal Farm against the pro-Soviet consensus

- **The choice.** Began *Animal Farm* November 1943, weeks after leaving the BBC — a satire of Stalinism at the exact peak of pro-Soviet wartime feeling, with the USSR an ally and Stalingrad fresh.
- **The rejections.** (1) Gollancz refused it. (2) Jonathan Cape accepted, then withdrew after "an important official" at the Ministry of Information warned him off; Cape's letter: "I think the choice of pigs as the ruling caste will no doubt give offence to many people, and particularly to anyone who is a bit touchy, as undoubtedly the Russians are." The official is identified by Timothy Garton Ash as "almost certainly" Peter Smollett (born Hans Peter Smolka), head of Soviet relations at the MOI — later exposed as a Soviet agent, codename ABO. (3) T.S. Eliot for Faber, 13 July 1944: "we have no conviction... that this is the right point of view from which to criticise the political situation at the present time"; the book's "view, which I take to be generally Trotskyite, is not convincing"; "what was needed, (someone might argue), was not more communism but more public-spirited pigs." (4) Dial Press, New York, reportedly on the grounds that animal stories don't sell in the USA. [SECONDARY: Wikipedia/Animal Farm synthesis; Open Culture & Poetry Foundation on the digitized Eliot letter; Garton Ash on Smollett; Dial anecdote EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED in detail]
- **His response.** Considered self-publishing it as a two-shilling pamphlet with a loan from David Astor [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Paul Potts's memoir]; wrote the furious preface "The Freedom of the Press" indicting voluntary self-censorship: "the sinister fact about literary censorship in England is that it is largely voluntary" [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED exact wording of that sentence] and, verified: "If liberty means anything at all it means the right to tell people what they do not want to hear." [PRIMARY: "The Freedom of the Press," intended preface to *Animal Farm*, written 1945 — verified against full text, orwell.ru. NOTE: this most-quoted Orwell line is from a text unpublished in his lifetime, found in 1971 and printed in the TLS, 15 September 1972.]
- Stuck with Secker & Warburg, the small anti-Stalinist house; published 17 August 1945; the book made him famous and solvent for the first time. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Taylor 2003]
- **Heuristic revealed.** When the consensus is at its most unanimous is precisely when the dissenting book must be written; measure censorship by what publishers refuse without being ordered to.

## 10. 1944-45: adopting Richard; Eileen's death

- **The adoption.** May-June 1944 the Orwells adopted a newborn, Richard Horatio Blair (born 14 May 1944) — Orwell, told he was probably sterile, wanted a child intensely. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Taylor 2003]
- **Eileen's death.** 29 March 1945: Eileen died under anaesthetic during a hysterectomy in Newcastle while Orwell was in Europe as a war correspondent for the Observer. She had chosen a cheaper provincial operation, her letters showing anxiety about cost. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Bowker 2003; Funder 2023 presses the cost point hardest]
- **His response.** Rushed back; within weeks returned to the continent to keep reporting; refused advice to give Richard up, hiring Susan Watson and later installing his sister Avril to help raise him. His grief ran opaque and practical; in 1945-46 he proposed marriage to several younger women (Celia Kirwan, Anne Popham, Sonia Brownell). To Anne Popham, April 1946: "What I am really asking you is if you would like to be the widow of a literary man." [PRIMARY: letter to Anne Popham, April 1946, *Complete Works*; quoted in D.J. Taylor, The Critic, 2020] ("whether you would like" in some transcriptions — variant recorded.)
- **Heuristic revealed.** Commitments to persons (the child) are non-negotiable even when circumstances collapse; grief is to be worked through by work. [INFERRED from conduct; he left almost no direct statement about Eileen's death — itself a datum, see §14]

## 11. 1946-48: Jura — trading health for Nineteen Eighty-Four

- **The choice.** Early 1946, to Dorothy Plowman: he was "smothered under journalism" (Davison titled the 1946 *Complete Works* volume *Smothered Under Journalism*). [PRIMARY phrase: letter to Dorothy Plowman, 1946] Fame after *Animal Farm* brought demands he wanted to escape; David Astor suggested Barnhill, a farmhouse at the roadless north end of Jura, Inner Hebrides — no electricity, no phone, a day's journey from a doctor — and Orwell, already visibly tubercular (a major haemorrhage in February 1946 he disguised from doctors as "gastritis"), moved there in May 1946. [SECONDARY: Taylor 2003 (the gastritis ruse); JSTOR Daily; Orwell Foundation "On Jura"]
- **The ordeal.** Drafted the novel through 1947; nearly drowned with Richard in the Corryvreckan whirlpool (August 1947) [SECONDARY: Crick 1980]; diagnosed December 1947; at Hairmyres hospital received Britain's scarce new streptomycin (obtained with Astor's money and, by several accounts, help clearing Treasury/import obstacles) from about 19-20 February 1948; developed a severe allergic reaction — dermatitis, stomatitis, throat ulcers, hair and nail loss — and the treatment stopped. "The leftover drug was given to two doctors' wives, both of whom recovered from their tuberculosis." [SECONDARY: "Down and almost out in Scotland," J R Soc Med / James Lind Library, 2005]
- **The fair copy.** Against medical logic he returned to Barnhill, and when no typist could be lured to the island, typed the final manuscript of *Nineteen Eighty-Four* himself, in bed, roughly 4,000 words a day, November-December 1948; he collapsed afterward and entered Cranham sanatorium in January 1949. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Taylor 2003; "The masterpiece that killed George Orwell" (Observer, Robert McCrum, 2009)]
- **CONTRADICTION RECORDED.** The romantic version — "he went to Jura to die/knowingly killed himself for the book" — is resisted by Crick and by Richard Blair (the son), who point out Jura was a family home project and the climate mild; the "masterpiece that killed him" framing is McCrum's, not a documented intention. [SECONDARY: both framings exist in the literature; Orwell himself wrote to friends that the island suited him]
- **Heuristic revealed.** Protect the one essential project by physically removing yourself from the attention economy, and pay whatever the body charges; when a scarce good runs surplus (streptomycin), hand it on.

## 12. 1949: the IRD list; refusing the Book-of-the-Month Club; the Henson statement

- **The list.** 6 April 1949, from his sickbed at Cranham, to Celia Kirwan of the Foreign Office's Information Research Department (an anti-communist, social-democratic propaganda unit — and a woman he had proposed to): "it isn't a bad idea to have the people who are probably unreliable listed" — offering names of writers who "should not be trusted as propagandists"; he sent 38 names from his private notebook of ~135 on 2 May 1949, asking that the list be returned as it was "libellous." [PRIMARY quotes: letters to Celia Kirwan, April-May 1949, via Timothy Garton Ash, "Orwell's List," NYRB 2003 (orwell.ru mirror); list published by the Guardian, 2003]
- **His reasoning, reconstructed.** He was not denouncing citizens to police; he was telling a propaganda department whom not to hire as its voices — people who would not fight Stalinism could not credibly write against it. He simultaneously opposed banning the Communist Party and supported due-process protections; Garton Ash's verdict: the act did not fundamentally contradict his principles because it was "white propaganda," not secret-police work — "you have to treat each case individually." [SECONDARY: Garton Ash 2003] Critics' verdict is the opposite: the man who invented the Thought Police "was a snitch" (Labour Heartlands headline; Alexander Cockburn's polemics call the list informing). [SECONDARY] — CONTRADICTION RECORDED; see §14.
- **The BOMC refusal.** March 1949: the US Book-of-the-Month Club asked to cut the Goldstein extracts and the Newspeak Appendix from *Nineteen Eighty-Four* — roughly a quarter of the text. Orwell to his agent Leonard Moore: "A book is built up as a balanced structure and one cannot simply remove large chunks here and there unless one is ready to recast the whole thing... I really cannot allow my work to be mucked about beyond a certain point, and I doubt whether it even pays in the long run." Warburg reckoned the refusal risked about £40,000. Three weeks later the Club yielded and took the book whole. [PRIMARY quote: letter to Moore, March 1949, *Complete Works*; SECONDARY circumstances: Warburg's account; Wikipedia/Nineteen Eighty-Four synthesis]
- **The Henson statement.** June 1949, after US press read the dying book as an attack on British Labour, statement to Francis A. Henson of the United Auto Workers (excerpts printed in Life, 25 July 1949, and the New York Times Book Review): "My recent novel is NOT intended as an attack on Socialism or on the British Labour Party (of which I am a supporter) but as a show-up of the perversions to which a centralised economy is liable... The moral to be drawn from this dangerous nightmare situation is a simple one: Don't let it happen. It depends on you." [PRIMARY: statement to Henson, *Complete Works* XX; wording as reproduced in multiple sources this session]
- **Heuristics revealed.** (a) Fight totalitarianism with named, accountable choices — and accept that clean hands are not on offer. (b) The work's integrity outranks its largest market. (c) When misread, issue the correction yourself, in plain terms, naming your own side ("of which I am a supporter").

## 13. Oct 1949-Jan 1950: marrying Sonia; the will

- **The marriage.** 13 October 1949: married Sonia Brownell (Horizon's editorial manager, who had earlier turned him down) in his room at University College Hospital, wearing a smoking jacket over pyjamas; David Astor best man. Motives attributed: will to live, care of the estate and of Richard; friends split between touched and appalled. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Taylor 2003; Bowker 2003]
- **The will (signed Eric Blair, 18 January 1950 — he died 21 January).** "I request that no memorial service be held for me after my death and that no biography of me shall be written." He also directed burial — not cremation — "according to the rites of the Church of England," despite lifelong unbelief. Astor got the atheist buried at All Saints', Sutton Courtenay (vicar Gordon Dunstan; a hesitant churchwarden reportedly won over by a copy of *Animal Farm*), under a stone naming only Eric Arthur Blair — no "George Orwell," no epitaph. [PRIMARY: will text as quoted; SECONDARY: Bowker, "George Orwell and the Church of England," Orwell Foundation; Orwell Society]
- **Sequel.** Sonia enforced the no-biography edict for two decades, then commissioned Muggeridge (who let it fail), later Crick (1980) — the ban ultimately broken by his own executor. [SECONDARY: Crick, "Orwell and Biography," LRB 1982; Orwell Society]
- **Heuristic revealed.** He chose the traditions of a country he loved over the doctrines he disbelieved (Anglican earth, English churchyard, real name); and tried — futilely — to keep the work separate from the life.

## 14. CONSISTENCY AUDIT — deeds vs words

**Where behavior matched preaching:**
- **Poverty and plain living.** Preached against acquisitiveness; lived it — resigned the imperial salary (1927), elective destitution (1928-31), spartan housekeeping even after *Animal Farm* money (Jura had no electricity). [PRIMARY on doctrine: *Wigan Pier*; SECONDARY on practice: Crick, Taylor]
- **Physical risk.** Preached engagement; enlisted in a militia at 33, took a bullet through the throat, ran errands past Assault Guards for a jailed comrade, tried to enlist in 1939 with ruined lungs. [PRIMARY: *Homage*; SECONDARY: Crick]
- **Truth-telling at career cost.** Barcelona reporting vs the left press (§6); *Animal Farm* at the pro-Soviet peak (§9); BOMC refusal (§12). Pattern across 12 years — this is his most consistent trait. [INFERRED from §§6, 9, 12]
- **Epistemic hygiene about himself.** "Beware of my partisanship, my mistakes of fact" (*Homage*); recording the exculpatory version of Smillie's death; the French-edition confession of selection. [PRIMARY]

**Where behavior contradicted preaching:**
- **The List vs the anti-informer ethic.** The author who made the informer the moral nadir of *Nineteen Eighty-Four*, who publicly opposed banning the CP and served the Freedom Defence Committee defending Communists' civil liberties, privately named 38 unreliables to a state propaganda arm — including guesses and gossip (annotations on Chaplin, Robeson, Priestley; slurs on some as "Jewish?" or homosexual). Defenders: Garton Ash — consistent, targeted, white-propaganda hiring advice. Prosecutors: Cockburn and others — a snitch's charter. Both positions stand in the record. [SECONDARY both ways: Garton Ash NYRB 2003; Labour Heartlands/Cockburn line]
- **Etonian vs classless.** Diagnosed himself "born into what you might describe as the lower-upper-middle class" [PRIMARY: *Wigan Pier* — verified against full text] and preached the abolition of class distinction, yet kept the drawling accent, the Home Counties manners, and put Richard's name down for Westminster School. [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Taylor 2003 (school detail EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED as to which school; Westminster per Taylor)]
- **Eileen vs "decency."** The prophet of common decency depended on Eileen's labor (she typed and by several accounts sharpened *Animal Farm*), was serially unfaithful, and in *Homage* never once names the wife who ran his supply line and saved his papers during the purge — she is "my wife" throughout. Anna Funder (*Wifedom*, 2023) builds the erasure case; critics (Michael Hofmann, ABR; others) counter that Funder reads through secondary sources and fictionalizes motive; D.J. Taylor (2023) offers "a man of his time." Eileen's fatal cheap surgery, chosen amid money worries while he spent on Jura plans, is the sharpest count. — CONTRADICTION RECORDED, unresolved in the literature. [SECONDARY: Funder 2023; Hofmann 2023; Taylor 2023]
- **"Documentary" that was shaped.** The famous Wigan image of the young woman kneeling at a blocked drain is "seen from the train" in the book, but his diary records passing her on foot in an alley — the scene was recomposed for art (noted by Crick). Whether "Shooting an Elephant" happened as told is contested: Davison found colonial-service testimony (George Stuart) that Blair was transferred after shooting an elephant; others note Orwell elsewhere called such pieces sketches; *Down and Out* rearranged time by his own admission (§2). [SECONDARY: Crick 1980; Davison apparatus; PRIMARY admission: French intro 1935]
- **Propaganda vs truth.** He wrote propaganda for two years at the BBC while privately noting (wartime diary) that all propaganda is lies — then judged the job "two wasted years" and left; the contradiction was one he ran consciously and terminated. [PRIMARY: resignation letter; EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED diary line]

**Net reading.** [INFERRED] His decisions cluster around one operation: *put the body where the claim is* — Burma guilt paid in tramping, socialism tested in a trench, censorship answered with an unsellable book, a dying body spent on a fair copy. The failures cluster where the claim was about people closest to him (Eileen) or where anti-totalitarian ends met state means (the List). He was consistent about institutions and causes; least consistent about the private, domestic, and self-mythologizing margins.

## 15. DECISION HEURISTICS EXTRACTED (index for the skill)

All [INFERRED] from the sections cited; quotes anchoring each are given above.

1. **Exit evil institutions; pay down the guilt in kind.** Resigning Burma, then choosing to live the condition of the oppressed rather than just regretting the oppression. (§1, §2)
2. **Go and see; the body is the instrument of knowledge.** Statistics and received opinion are hypotheses until tested by physical presence — tramping, the mine, the trench, the hospital. (§2, §4, §5)
3. **Act first inside the event, theorize after.** "It seemed the only conceivable thing to do" — joining the militia within days; the writing came later. (§5)
4. **Update allegiance the moment the evidence turns, publicly, against your own convenience.** Planned International Column transfer reversed within days of the May Days. (§5)
5. **Decide by the concrete instance, not the abstraction.** The flesh-and-blood worker vs the policeman; case-by-case judgment even in the IRD affair ("treat each case individually"). (§5, §12)
6. **Publish what you saw even when your own side's gatekeepers refuse it**; accept ostracism and commercial failure as the price of the record. (§4, §6, §9)
7. **Stamp your own account with its error bars.** "Beware of my partisanship, my mistakes of fact"; recording the version of Smillie's death that weakened his own case. (§5, §6)
8. **Trust the gut as data about your real beliefs.** The pact-night dream that told him he was "patriotic at heart" and would fight — instinct overruling nine years of doctrine, admitted in print. (§7)
9. **Serve a compromised machine only with a red line and an exit audit.** BBC: never say on air what you wouldn't say as a private individual; leave when the work "produces no result." (§8)
10. **Write against the consensus precisely when it is most unanimous** — and if every publisher refuses, prepare to publish it yourself. (§9)
11. **The work's integrity outranks its largest market.** Refusing the Book-of-the-Month Club cut at a risked £40,000. (§12)
12. **Correct misreadings yourself, in plain words, naming your own side.** The Henson statement: "NOT intended as an attack on Socialism... Don't let it happen. It depends on you." (§12)
13. **Personal loyalty is spent in person and at risk** — the War Department gamble for Kopp while himself hunted; keeping Richard after Eileen died. (§5, §10)
14. **Remove yourself physically from the attention economy to protect the essential project** — Jura against journalism, telephone, and fame; accept the bodily cost knowingly or not. (§11)
15. **Anti-model (from his failures):** the same man under-audited what was nearest him — the wife whose labor he consumed unnamed, the informer's role he accepted when the enemy was hateful enough, the shaped anecdote passed off as plain record. Vigilance pointed outward is not vigilance. (§14)
