# George Orwell — Dimension 2: Dialogic & Occasional Thinking
## How he argued on his feet, replied when challenged, and changed his mind

**Scope:** Tribune "As I Please" columns (1943-47); London Letters to Partisan Review (1941-46); letters (Willmett, Gollancz, Koestler, Macdonald, Henson statement); polemical exchanges (pacifists, Comfort, Wells, Kingsley Martin); BBC years; diaries vs public writing; dated position changes; refusals and silences.

**Method note:** Quotations marked [PRIMARY] were checked against full texts fetched during this session (telelib.com etext of the Tribune columns, orwell.ru, the Boston University Partisan Review scan archive, laban.rs and george-orwell.org mirrors, the Orwell Diaries blog which serialises Peter Davison's edition, and facsimile reprints of letters). [SECONDARY] = biographer/critic claim, named. [INFERRED] = my inference, with basis. [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED] = from model knowledge, not checked against a full text this session. Orwell is heavily misquoted; nothing here comes from quote-aggregator sites.

## 1. "As I Please" — Tribune columns, Dec 1943 to Feb 1945 (resumed Nov 1946 - Apr 1947)

His most improvisational form: a weekly column of 3-6 unconnected items separated by dots, moving without transition from geopolitics to rose bushes to reader quizzes.

**The very first column (3 Dec 1943) opens as reported dialogue, not argument** — a drunk American soldier in a tobacconist's shop: "Soldier: 'Wharrishay is, perfijious Albion. You heard that? Perfijious Albion. Never trust a Britisher.' / Orwell: 'Can't trust them with what?'" He then generalises: "it is difficult to go anywhere in London without having the feeling that Britain is now Occupied Territory," and turns the item into a plea against censorship-by-tact: "it is just because one does want a good relationship between the two countries that one wants plain speaking." [PRIMARY — As I Please, Tribune, 3 Dec 1943, full text verified]

**Recurring habits of argument observed across the verified columns:**
- **Starts from a physical object or overheard scene** (a leaflet passed in a pub, a pamphlet, a sixpenny rose bush, a shop encounter), then climbs to a general principle. [INFERRED — from the verified columns of 3 Dec 1943, 21 Jan 1944, 24 Mar 1944, 1 Sep 1944, 8 Dec 1944]
- **Concedes the strongest available point to the other side before attacking** — e.g. on the Warsaw Rising charges: "No shadow of proof is offered for any of these charges, though 1 and 2 are of a kind that could be verified and may well be true. My own guess is that 2 is true and 1 partly true." Then: "The third charge makes nonsense of the first two." [PRIMARY — As I Please, 1 Sep 1944]
- **Names his own past guilt inside the attack** (see the "objectively" retraction below).
- **Definition-by-usage instead of definition-by-theory:** "as used, the word 'Fascism' is almost entirely meaningless... I have heard it applied to farmers, shopkeepers, Social Credit, corporal punishment, fox-hunting, bull-fighting, the 1922 Committee, the 1941 Committee, Kipling, Gandhi, Chiang Kai-Shek, homosexuality, Priestley's broadcasts, Youth Hostels, astrology, women, dogs and I do not know what else... almost any English person would accept 'bully' as a synonym for 'Fascist'... use the word with a certain amount of circumspection and not, as is usually done, degrade it to the level of a swearword." [PRIMARY — "What is Fascism?", As I Please, 24 Mar 1944]
- **Direct engagement with hostile readers, printed in the column itself.** 21 Jan 1944: "A CORRESPONDENT reproaches me with being 'negative' and 'always attacking things'. The fact is that we live in a time when causes for rejoicing are not numerous. But I like praising things, when there is anything to praise, and I would like here to write a few lines... in praise of the Woolworth's Rose... I had planted both of those in 1936. And I thought, 'All that for sixpence!'" [PRIMARY — As I Please, 21 Jan 1944]
- **Pre-empting the mailbag.** Closing the Warsaw column: "Finally, a word to the people who will write me letters about this. May I once again draw attention to the title of this column and remind everyone that the Editors of Tribune are not necessarily in agreement with all that I say, but are putting into practice their belief in freedom of speech?" [PRIMARY — As I Please, 1 Sep 1944]
- **Sudden topic shifts as method:** the 8 Dec 1944 column moves from the ethics of the word "objectively" → a satirical Irish-American leaflet ("The first American son-of-a-bitch to get four new tyres from the Ration Board was Abie Goldstein") → a running dispute with a Mr Martin Walter's fiction-writing "Plot Formula" school → a literary puzzle answer. [PRIMARY — column verified in full]
- **Crowdsourcing his readers:** he announces himself "an industrious collector of pamphlets" and repeatedly recommends, quizzes ("WHO wrote this?"), and solicits material from readers. [PRIMARY — 3 Dec 1943 and 8 Dec 1944 columns]

**Abuse received and processed into essays:** "I know by experience that a favourable reference to 'Nature' in one of my articles is liable to bring me abusive letters, and though the key-word in these letters is usually 'sentimental', two ideas seem to be mixed up in them." His reply: "Is it wicked to take a pleasure in spring...? is it politically reprehensible... to point out that life is frequently more worth living because of a blackbird's song, a yellow elm tree in October, or some other natural phenomenon which does not cost money and does not have what the editors of left-wing newspapers call a class angle?... The atom bombs are piling up in the factories, the police are prowling through the cities, the lies are streaming from the loudspeakers, but the earth is still going round the sun, and neither the dictators nor the bureaucrats, deeply as they disapprove of the process, are able to prevent it." [PRIMARY — "Some Thoughts on the Common Toad", Tribune, 12 Apr 1946, full text verified]

## 2. London Letters to Partisan Review (1941-46): auditing his own predictions

Fifteen "London Letters" to the New York quarterly, spring 1941 to summer 1946. The critical document is the **London Letter in Partisan Review, Winter 1945 (vol. 12 no. 1, written Dec 1944)**, where he formally audits his own record — verified against the BU scan of the printed issue:

- "**I fell into the trap of assuming that 'the war and the revolution are inseparable'**" — his named "very great error": having written (from 1940 to mid-1942) that "Either we turn this into a revolutionary war, or we lose it," which "coloured my judgment" and made him exaggerate both political instability and the depth of wartime social change in Britain. [PRIMARY — London Letter, PR Winter 1945, pp. 79-80]
- "**It seems to me very important to realize that we have been wrong, and say so.** Most people nowadays, when their predictions are falsified, just impudently claim that they have been justified, and squeeze the facts accordingly." [PRIMARY — same letter]
- He lists his own falsified claims by name — "Laissez-faire capitalism is dead", "With its present social structure England cannot survive" — and notes that every faction "all claim... that their prophecies and no others have been borne out by events." [PRIMARY — same letter]
- He also certifies that he was not tailoring output for a foreign audience: he had expressed "the same ideas, and much more violently, in books and articles only published at home." [PRIMARY — same letter]
- On the war-entry debate generally: "we were all wrong" — the whole intelligentsia, across camps. [PRIMARY — same letter, as rendered by the BU page scan]

**The self-audit habit generalised:** the same diagnostic appears the same week in Tribune: "It is this habit of mind [refusing to grant an opponent honesty], among other things, that has made political prediction in our time so remarkably unsuccessful." [PRIMARY — As I Please, 8 Dec 1944] And later in "Second Thoughts on James Burnham" (Polemic, 1946) he generalises it into a rule: the power-worshipper predicts the continuation of whatever is happening now. [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED — essay title and argument well established; exact wording not fetched this session]

## 3. Letters: thinking addressed to one person

**To Noel Willmett, 18 May 1944** (the 1984 blueprint letter; full text verified from facsimile reprint):
- "I must say I believe, or fear, that taking the world as a whole these things are on the increase. Hitler, no doubt, will soon disappear, but only at the expense of strengthening (a) Stalin, (b) the Anglo-American millionaires and (c) all sorts of petty fuhrers of the type of de Gaulle." Fuhrer-figures named: "Hitler, Stalin, Salazar, Franco, **Gandhi**, De Valera are all varying examples."
- "Already history has in a sense ceased to exist, ie. there is no such thing as a history of our own times which could be universally accepted... Hitler can say that the Jews started the war, and if he survives that will become official history. He can't say that two and two are five, because for the purposes of, say, ballistics they have to make four. But if the sort of world that I am afraid of arrives, a world of two or three great superstates which are unable to conquer one another, two and two could become five if the fuhrer wished it. That, so far as I can see, is the direction in which we are actually moving, though, of course, the process is reversible."
- On Britain: "the intellectuals are more totalitarian in outlook than the common people. On the whole the English intelligentsia have opposed Hitler, but only at the price of accepting Stalin."
- His operating rule: "If one simply proclaims that all is for the best and doesn't point to the sinister symptoms, one is merely helping to bring totalitarianism nearer." And on the war: "It is a choice of evils — I fancy nearly every war is that... we have to keep on making it the better, which involves constant criticism." [ALL PRIMARY — letter to Noel Willmett, 18 May 1944; note "history stopped in 1936" is NOT in this letter — see Koestler below]

**"History stopped in 1936" — actually said in conversation to Arthur Koestler**, reported by Orwell himself: "I remarked to Arthur Koestler, 'History stopped in 1936', at which he nodded in immediate understanding. We were both thinking of totalitarianism in general, but more particularly of the Spanish civil war." Surrounding argument: "I saw, in fact, history being written not in terms of what happened but of what ought to have happened according to various 'party lines'"; and the endpoint: "If the Leader says of such and such an event, 'It never happened' — well, it never happened. If he says that two and two are five — well, two and two are five. This prospect frightens me much more than bombs." [PRIMARY — "Looking Back on the Spanish War", 1943, text verified at orwell.ru]

**To Dwight Macdonald, 5 Dec 1946, explaining Animal Farm** (verified via the NYRB reprint of the letter): he "intended it primarily as a satire on the Russian revolution" but with wider application: "that kind of revolution (violent conspiratorial revolution, led by unconsciously power-hungry people) can only lead to a change of masters... **revolutions only effect a radical improvement when the masses are alert and know how to chuck out their leaders as soon as the latter have done their job.** The turning-point of the story was supposed to be when the pigs kept the milk and apples for themselves (Kronstadt). If the other animals had had the sense to put their foot down then, it would have been all right... **You can't have a revolution unless you make it for yourself; there is no such thing as a benevolent dictat[or]ship.**" [PRIMARY — letter to Macdonald, Dec 1946]

**The Francis A. Henson statement on Nineteen Eighty-Four (16 June 1949)** — written for Henson of the United Automobile Workers, released through publisher Fredric Warburg after US readers (and Life/NY Daily News coverage) read the novel as anti-socialist: "My recent novel is NOT intended as an attack on Socialism or on the British Labour Party (of which I am a supporter) but as a show-up of the perversions to which a centralized economy is liable and which have already been partly realized in Communism and Fascism... totalitarian ideas have taken root in the minds of intellectuals everywhere, and I have tried to draw these ideas out to their logical consequences... **The moral to be drawn from this dangerous nightmare situation is a simple one: Don't let it happen. It depends on you.**" [PRIMARY — statement to F.A. Henson, June 1949; text as given in Davison CW XX and reproduced by Open Culture/gatwickcitytimes; Wikipedia's Nineteen Eighty-Four article concurs. Caveat per Davison: the statement survives via press summaries/amalgamations, so wording is a reconstruction — worth flagging.]

**To Victor Gollancz:** Gollancz commissioned The Road to Wigan Pier then prefixed the 1937 Left Book Club edition with a foreword disowning its second half; he refused Homage to Catalonia before a word was written (it "controverted" the Popular Front line); he rejected Animal Farm in 1944; in March-April 1947 Orwell wrote asking to be released from his two-novel contract, and Gollancz released him. [SECONDARY — Crick 1980, Taylor 2003; the 1937 foreword itself is printed in LBC copies] Orwell to Gollancz on the Nazi-Soviet-pact left, 8 Jan 1940: "The intellectuals who are at present pointing out that democracy and fascism are the same thing depress me horribly." [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED — cited in Davison CW XII; wording not checked against facsimile this session]

**To/with Arthur Koestler:** beyond "History stopped in 1936", Orwell reviewed Darkness at Noon (New Statesman, 1941) as the essential book on the Moscow confessions; spent Christmas 1945 at Koestler's cottage in Wales, where the two drafted plans and a manifesto for a revived League for the Rights of Man / freedom-of-thought organisation (it fed into Orwell's essay "Politics and the English Language" period activism and the Freedom Defence Committee). [SECONDARY — Crick 1980; Michael Scammell, Koestler, 2009; David Cesarani 1998]

## 4. Polemical exchanges: how he behaved when challenged

**vs the pacifists — "Pacifism and the War" (Partisan Review, Sep-Oct 1942).** Replying jointly to D.S. Savage, George Woodcock and Alex Comfort, who had attacked his London Letters:
- The formula: "**Pacifism is objectively pro-Fascist. This is elementary common sense.** If you hamper the war effort of one side you automatically help that of the other." And: "In practice, 'he that is not with me is against me'."
- Turning an opponent's own concession into a weapon: Savage said a German pacifist would be "objectively pro-British"; Orwell: "But of course he would be! That is why pacifist activities are not permitted in those countries."
- Challenged by Woodcock over his BBC job (Woodcock had jeered that Orwell was doing imperial propaganda): "Does Mr Woodcock really know what kind of stuff I put out in the Indian broadcasts? He does not."
- On Comfort's debating manner: "Instead of answering what I have said he tries to prejudice an audience... That is... not what I mean by intellectual honesty." [ALL PRIMARY — text verified at orwell.ru]

**The partial retraction, As I Please, 8 Dec 1944** — the key self-correction, in full: "We are told that it is only people's objective actions that matter, and their subjective feelings are of no importance. Thus pacifists, by obstructing the war effort, are 'objectively' aiding the Nazis; and therefore the fact that they may be personally hostile to Fascism is irrelevant. **I have been guilty of saying this myself more than once.** The same argument is applied to Trotskyism... This is not only dishonest; it also carries a severe penalty with it. **If you disregard people's motives, it becomes much harder to foresee their actions.**... In my opinion a few pacifists are inwardly pro-Nazi, and extremist left-wing parties will inevitably contain Fascist spies. The important thing is to discover which individuals are honest and which are not, and the usual blanket accusation merely makes this more difficult. The atmosphere of hatred in which controversy is conducted blinds people to considerations of this kind. To admit that an opponent might be both honest and intelligent is felt to be intolerable." [PRIMARY — full column verified] NOTE: this is a retraction of the *method* (motive-blind "objectively" reasoning), not of his anti-pacifism — he still says "a few pacifists are inwardly pro-Nazi." Secondary sources that call it a flat repudiation (e.g. studentactivism.net's "Orwell Repudiates") overstate slightly; Reason's 2014 piece ("Why Selectively Quoting Orwell Matters") gets the nuance right. [INFERRED — from the verified text]

**vs Alex Comfort in verse (Tribune, June 1943).** Comfort, as "Obadiah Hornbooke", published "Letter to an American Visitor"; Orwell answered in kind with "As One Non-Combatant to Another (A Letter to 'Obadiah Hornbooke')" — ~160 lines of Byronic ottava rima attacking pacifist aesthetes ("each one hopes... to hear the beating of its angel's wings" is Comfort's side; Orwell's reply includes the thrust "Your hands are clean, and so were Pontius Pilate's" [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED — line widely cited; poem's existence, venue, form PRIMARY/SECONDARY via D.J. Taylor, Orwell Foundation, and Orwell Society "The Comfort of Pacifism"]). Orwell insisted Tribune print Comfort's attack in the first place — dialogic even in vendetta. Both poems were anthologised by Philip Larkin in the Oxford Book of Twentieth-Century English Verse (1973). [SECONDARY — Taylor, Orwell Foundation article on Orwell's poetry] Afterwards Orwell and Comfort corresponded on friendly terms and Comfort contributed to Tribune while Orwell was literary editor. [SECONDARY — Orwell Society]

**vs H.G. Wells (1941-42).** Orwell's "Wells, Hitler and the World State" (Horizon, Aug 1941), verified text: "Hitler is a criminal lunatic, and Hitler has an army of millions of men... The energy that actually shapes the world springs from emotions — racial pride, leader-worship, religious belief, love of war — which liberal intellectuals mechanically write off as anachronisms... The people who say that Hitler is Antichrist, or alternatively, the Holy Ghost, are nearer an understanding of the truth than the intellectuals who for ten dreadful years have kept it up that he is merely a figure out of comic opera... Much of what Wells has imagined and worked for is physically there in Nazi Germany... but all in the service of ideas appropriate to the Stone Age... **Wells is too sane to understand the modern world.**" Note the pattern: devastating attack wrapped in filial homage — "Thinking people who were born about the beginning of this century are in some sense Wells's own creation... But how much it is, after all, to have any talents to squander." [PRIMARY — orwell.ru text] The aftermath: a "God-awful row" at dinner witnessed by Inez Holden; after Orwell's BBC talk "The Re-discovery of Europe" reprised the criticism, Orwell's own diary for 27 Mar 1942 records: "[Abusive letter from H.G. Wells, who addresses me as 'You shit', among other things.]" [PRIMARY — wartime diary, 27.3.42, Davison edition via Orwell Diaries blog; the bracketed italic form suggests a later interpolation in the diary — flagged. Context SECONDARY — Davison's footnote; Holden's account via Crick 1980.]

**vs Kingsley Martin / New Statesman (1937 onward).** Martin's NS refused Orwell's eyewitness reporting from Barcelona in 1937, and then his review of Borkenau's The Spanish Cockpit, on the stated ground that it "controverts the political policy of the paper" (no enemies on the Left); a kill fee was paid. Orwell never forgave him. Malcolm Muggeridge's much-repeated account: at a Soho lunch years later Orwell asked Muggeridge to swap seats "so that he didn't have to look at that **corrupt face**" when Martin sat within view. [SECONDARY — Muggeridge's recollections (e.g. in his Orwell memoir pieces), retold by Crick 1980, RealClearHistory 2021, orwelltoday.com] In Orwell's private 1949 notebook of crypto-communists and fellow-travellers, Martin's entry reads (per Davison CW XX): "Decayed liberal. Very dishonest." [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED — wording widely cited from CW XX; not checked against the printed volume this session]

## 5. BBC Eastern Service, Aug 1941 - Nov 1943

- Talks producer/broadcaster to India; wrote weekly war commentaries; invented formats (the "Voice" poetry magazine programme). The scripts show him practising honest propaganda under censorship — and privately doubting it (see diaries below). [SECONDARY — Davison, "Orwell at the BBC: Two Wasted Years?", Orwell Society; W.J. West, Orwell: The War Broadcasts, 1985]
- **Resignation letter to L.F. Rushbrook Williams, 24 Sep 1943:** not a protest — he stressed no disagreement with policy, no grievance, generous treatment, and: "**On no occasion have I been compelled to say on the air anything that I would not have said as a private individual.**" But he was leaving because he was "wasting my own time and the public money on doing work that produces no result... I believe that in the present political situation the broadcasting of British propaganda to India is an almost hopeless task" — and he could be more useful writing. [PRIMARY — resignation letter, quoted by Davison (Orwell Society) and In These Times; the "wasting my own time and the public money" phrase verified in both]
- **His flat verdict, to Philip Rahv, 9 Dec 1943:** "I have left the BBC after two wasted years." [PRIMARY — letter to Rahv, CW XVI/2390, quoted by Davison]
- Davison's counter-assessment: the years were "decidedly not wasted" — formidable output, long-term influence (he argues Orwell helped inspire what became the Third Programme). [SECONDARY — Davison, Orwell Society article] CONTRADICTION preserved: Orwell's own verdict ("two wasted years") vs his editor's ("not wasted") — and both vs the fact that Nineteen Eighty-Four's Ministry of Truth canteen, censorship indexes, and (by legend) Room 101 — named after a BBC conference room — drew on the experience. [SECONDARY — W.J. West; the Room 101 story is repeated lore, plausible but soft]

## 6. Diaries: private processing vs public voice

- **Wartime diary, 14 Mar 1942 (on his own BBC newsletters, alleging Japanese designs he did not believe):** "**All propaganda is lies, even when one is telling the truth. I don't think this matters so long as one knows what one is doing, and why.**" Same entry: the BBC atmosphere is "halfway between a girls' school and a lunatic asylum," the work "useless, or slightly worse than useless." [PRIMARY — diary 14.3.42, Orwell Diaries blog/Davison] **Divergence:** five months later, publicly, he defended the same broadcasts against Woodcock ("Does Mr Woodcock really know what kind of stuff I put out...?"). Privately corrosive, publicly defensive — he distinguished *knowing* propaganda is lies from *conceding the point to an opponent*. [INFERRED — from the two verified texts]
- **Wartime diary, 27 Apr 1942:** "**We are all drowning in filth.** When I talk to anyone or read the writings of anyone who has any axe to grind, I feel that intellectual honesty and balanced judgement have simply disappeared from the face of the earth. Everyone's thought is forensic... sympathy can be turned on and off like a tap according to political expediency... But is there no one who has both firm opinions and a balanced outlook? Actually there are plenty, but they are all powerless. **All power is in the hands of paranoiacs.**" [PRIMARY — diary 27.4.42, verified] This private despair became, laundered and cooled, the public opening of the 8 Dec 1944 As I Please ("Nobody is searching for the truth, everybody is putting forward a 'case'..."). The diary is the rehearsal room; the column is the performance. [INFERRED — direct verbal overlap between the two verified texts]
- **Hop-Picking diary (Aug-Oct 1931):** raw field notes on wages, tally rates, and casual-ward slang, later reworked into the essay "Hop-Picking" (New Statesman) and the Trafalgar Square/hop-fields chapters of A Clergyman's Daughter — his standing method: notebook first, persona later. [SECONDARY — Davison, Diaries (2009); Orwell Foundation diaries introduction]
- **Domestic diaries (1938-40, 1946-48):** obsessive daily records of eggs laid, weather, garden work — almost no politics; kept simultaneously with the most political journalism in England. The last diary entry (Sept 1949, University College Hospital) merely "sets out the daily routines" of the ward. [SECONDARY — Orwell Foundation substack on the Diaries; D.J. Taylor]
- **The last notebook aphorism:** "At fifty, everyone has the face he deserves." CONTRADICTION on record: commonly cited (after Davison CW XX) as the final entry of his last literary notebook, dated 17 April 1949; the Cambridge Introduction to George Orwell excerpt instead says it was penned "just days before he died" (January 1950) and renders it "After 50...". Wording and dating both unstable across sources; the CW XX version is the one to trust. [SECONDARY — conflicting renderings recorded, not harmonised]

## 7. Position changes, dated, with before/after evidence

1. **Semi-pacifism → pro-war, overnight, Aug 1939.** BEFORE: "For several years the coming war was a nightmare to me, and at times I even made speeches and wrote pamphlets against it." AFTER: "the night before the Russo-German pact was announced I dreamed that the war had started... It taught me two things, first, that I should be simply relieved when the long-dreaded war started, secondly, that **I was patriotic at heart, would not sabotage or act against my own side, would support the war, would fight in it if possible**... I came downstairs to find the newspaper announcing Ribbentrop's flight to Moscow." He is explicit that reasons came after the verdict: "I don't pretend that that is the emotional basis of my actions." [PRIMARY — "My Country Right or Left", Folios of New Writing, autumn 1940; full text verified]
2. **"Revolution or defeat" (1940-41) → quietly abandoned, formally audited Dec 1944.** BEFORE: "Only revolution can save England, that has been obvious for years... I dare say the London gutters will have to run with blood. All right, let them, if it is necessary. But when the red militias are billeted in the Ritz I shall still feel that the England I was taught to love so long ago... is somehow persisting" [PRIMARY — My Country Right or Left, 1940]; The Lion and the Unicorn (Feb 1941) argued war and revolution were inseparable. AFTER: "I fell into the trap of assuming that 'the war and the revolution are inseparable'... It seems to me very important to realize that we have been wrong, and say so." [PRIMARY — London Letter, PR Winter 1945]
3. **Gandhi: "petty fuhrer" (1944) → "how clean a smell" (1949).** BEFORE: Gandhi listed among fuhrer-examples alongside Hitler, Stalin, Salazar, Franco, De Valera. [PRIMARY — Willmett letter, 18 May 1944] AFTER: "Saints should always be judged guilty until they are proved innocent..."; "I have never been able to feel much liking for Gandhi, but I do not feel sure that as a political thinker he was wrong in the main, nor do I believe that his life was a failure"; closing: "regarded simply as a politician, and compared with the other leading political figures of our time, **how clean a smell he has managed to leave behind!**" [PRIMARY — "Reflections on Gandhi", Partisan Review, Jan 1949; key passages verified] The scepticism never fully dissolves — the essay still calls sainthood "anti-human" — softening, not conversion. [INFERRED — from the essay's structure]
4. **"Objectively pro-Fascist" (1942) → retraction of the method (Dec 1944).** Documented in full in section 4. The dates matter: the formula and its abandonment are 27 months apart, and the retraction names his own guilt.
5. **Sneering at crank-vegetarian-pacifist culture (1936-42) → institutional collaboration with the same people (1944-49).** BEFORE: Wigan Pier's "every fruit-juice drinker, nudist, sandal-wearer, sex-maniac, Quaker, 'Nature Cure' quack, pacifist and feminist in England" [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED exact list order — Road to Wigan Pier ch. 11, 1937]; the 1942 savaging of Savage/Woodcock/Comfort [PRIMARY]. AFTER: from 1945 Orwell served as vice-chairman of the Freedom Defence Committee (chair: the anarchist-adjacent Herbert Read; secretary circle included Woodcock), defending pacifists' and anarchists' civil liberties; Woodcock became a friend and wrote the sympathetic study The Crystal Spirit (1966). [SECONDARY — Woodcock 1966; Crick 1980] Woodcock's testimony is that Orwell kept polemic and friendship in separate compartments. [SECONDARY — The Crystal Spirit]
6. **The USSR alliance: wartime "lesser evil" support → refusal of the intelligentsia's silence.** 1944, same season, both verified: "I would support the USSR against Germany because... it retains enough of the original ideas of the Revolution to make it a more hopeful phenomenon than Nazi Germany" [PRIMARY — Willmett letter] AND "Do remember that dishonesty and cowardice always have to be paid for. Don't imagine that for years on end you can make yourself the boot-licking propagandist of the Soviet régime, or any other régime, and then suddenly return to mental decency. **Once a whore, always a whore.**" [PRIMARY — As I Please, 1 Sep 1944, on the press and the Warsaw Rising]. Not a contradiction to him: support the alliance, refuse the lie — "our cause is the better, but we have to keep on making it the better, which involves constant criticism." [PRIMARY — Willmett letter]

## 8. What he refused to discuss or do

- **Eileen O'Shaughnessy's death (29 Mar 1945, under anaesthetic): near-total public silence.** He was in Europe as a war correspondent; he announced it to friends in flat, controlled letters (to Lydia Jackson, Anthony Powell and others, early April 1945) and never treated it in a published essay; the diaries are effectively silent. [SECONDARY — Crick 1980; Bowker 2003; the letters are in Davison, A Life in Letters] CONTRADICTION on record: Anna Funder's Wifedom (2023) reads the silence as part of a career-long erasure of Eileen's labour and mind ("patriarchy's magic trick"); reviewers including D.J. Taylor and several Orwell scholars replied that Funder overreads, mistakes period convention and Orwell's general reticence for singular callousness, and occasionally fictionalises. Both positions stand; neither is refuted. [SECONDARY — Funder 2023; Taylor and press responses 2023]
- **Refused to soften Animal Farm (1944-45), at real cost.** Gollancz rejected it; Jonathan Cape accepted, then withdrew after a Ministry of Information official (later identified as Peter Smollett, subsequently revealed as a Soviet agent) warned him off — Cape's letter worried that "the choice of pigs as the ruling caste will no doubt give offence... particularly to anyone who is a bit touchy, as undoubtedly the Russians are," and floated making the rulers some other animal; T.S. Eliot for Faber declined it, conceding the skill but observing "your pigs are far more intelligent than the other animals... what was needed (someone might argue) was not more communism but more public-spirited pigs." Orwell changed nothing and wrote the furious intended preface "The Freedom of the Press" (unpublished until 1972): "**If liberty means anything at all, it means the right to tell people what they do not want to hear.**" [SECONDARY for the rejection letters — Cape and Eliot letters, printed in Davison CW XVI and widely reproduced (BBC, Guardian); PRIMARY for the preface sentence — "The Freedom of the Press", first pub. TLS 15 Sep 1972]
- **Kept his religious feeling opaque.** Publicly a materialist and scourge of Anglo-Catholic literati; privately he attended and took Communion at his parish church in the early 1930s while telling Eleanor Jaques it seemed "rather mean to go to HC [Holy Communion] when one doesn't believe" but he had "passed myself off for pious" [EMBEDDED-UNVERIFIED — letter to Eleanor Jaques, autumn 1932, in Davison]; he married (1936) and was remarried (1949) with Church of England rites, and his will directed burial "according to the rites of the Church of England" — arranged by Powell and Muggeridge at All Saints', Sutton Courtenay. No public essay ever squares this; he declined to explain it. [SECONDARY — Crick 1980; Taylor 2003; Powell's and Muggeridge's memoirs]
- **Refused to treat opponents' motives as settled even while fighting them** — post-Dec 1944 this became explicit doctrine: "The important thing is to discover which individuals are honest and which are not." [PRIMARY — As I Please, 8 Dec 1944]
- **Refused (in his own account) to say on air what he wouldn't say as a private individual** — the BBC resignation letter's one boast. [PRIMARY — resignation letter, 24 Sep 1943]

## Contradictions register (kept, not harmonized)

1. **"Two wasted years" (Orwell, letter to Rahv, Dec 1943) vs "decidedly not wasted" (Davison, Orwell Society).**
2. **"At fifty, everyone has the face he deserves":** dated 17 Apr 1949 (common CW-based citation) vs "just days before he died," Jan 1950, as "After 50..." (Cambridge Introduction to George Orwell excerpt). Wording and date unstable.
3. **The 8 Dec 1944 "objectively" passage:** billed by some (studentactivism.net) as a repudiation of "pacifism is objectively pro-Fascist"; the verified text retracts the *reasoning style* while restating "a few pacifists are inwardly pro-Nazi."
4. **Eileen's erasure:** Funder (2023) vs Taylor et al. (2023) — see section 8.
5. **Henson statement wording:** presented everywhere as verbatim Orwell; Davison's apparatus notes it survives through Warburg's press-release channel and partly via press summaries — treat "It depends on you" as canonical but the frame as mediated.
6. **Orwell on his own consistency:** he claimed (PR Winter 1945) he said the same things at home "much more violently" — true for 1940-42 revolutionism, but his 1942 "objectively pro-Fascist" polemic appeared in the American PR while the retraction ran in the domestic Tribune; the audiences for attack and correction were not identical. [INFERRED — from publication venues]

## What this dimension yields for a thinking framework

- **Audit your own predictions on a schedule, in public, by name** ("It seems to me very important to realize that we have been wrong, and say so").
- **Retract methods, not just conclusions** (the "objectively" passage retracts a *form of argument*).
- **Concede the strongest hostile point first; quantify your own credence** ("2 is true and 1 partly true").
- **Use the concrete object as the unit of thought** (rose bush, leaflet, toad — then the principle).
- **Print your hecklers and answer them in the same column.**
- **Keep private despair and public argument in separate registers, but let the diary feed the column.**
- **Separate alliance from assent:** support the lesser evil "which involves constant criticism."
- **Track the trajectory, not the snapshot:** Gandhi 1944 "petty fuhrer" → 1949 "how clean a smell"; Comfort 1942 target → 1946 collaborator.
